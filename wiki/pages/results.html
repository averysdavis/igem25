Verification of Transformants

We performed chemical transformation on two tubes of wild E. coli K12, one of which we added our plasmid pET-csg-sazCA-amp. This plasmid would express a curli fiber and carbonic anhydrase fusion protein under iPTG induction, and also made our bacteria ampicillin resistant. We plated our recovered transformants onto LB plates and LB/amp plates and let them incubate overnight at 37C. .

Top left: LB/amp plates, K12 without added DNA Top right: LB/amp plate, K12 with added DNA Bottom left: LB plate, K12 without added DNA Bottom right: LB plate, K12 with added DNA

As hypothesized, for our bacteria without plasmid DNA, there was a smear on the LB plate and no bacteria on the LB/amp plate. For our bacteria with plasmid DNA, there was a smear on the LB plate and individual colonies on the LB/amp plate. This confirmed that our ampicillin was working properly, and that our E. coli K12 had likely been transformed. We also performed Colony PCR as well as sequenced transformants of our part (link to iGEM parts page). Both procedures supported successful transformation of our E. Coli K-12.

Production of Curli Fibers

Curli fibers are extracellular proteinaceous fibers with amyloid folds that create a biofilm. In total, there are seven curli-specific genes (csg) that form two operons, csgBAC and csgDEFG. csgA and csgB are the structural subunits of curli, wherein csgB anchors to the outer membrane while csgA is the major curli fiber subunit. csgC is a periplasmic chaperone, csgE, csgF, and csgE form the secretion assembly machinery, and csgD a transcriptional activator of the csgBAC operon. Figure 2 from Bhoite et al. (2019) shows the curli secretion pathway.

To test expression of our modified curli protein (link to igem parts page (or any other pages)), we utilized a congo red fluorescence assay assay inspired by the Joshi lab, as well as a spin down congo red assay from iGEM team Delft-Leiden. Our first step was to test a variety of inducer concentration to find what optimized expression.

Comparison of varying iPTG concentrations on absorbance after spin down of transformant culture. More negative absorbance indicates greater presence of Curli fibers. Graph taken from 8/17 curli absorbance assay. Comparison of varying iPTG concentrations on fluorescence at 625 nm after excitation at 525 nm. Greater fluorescence indicates greater presence of Curli fibers. Graph taken from 8/17 curli fluorescence assay.

From these results, we decided to proceed with an inducer concentration of 0.2 mM, and ran a 96 sample assay in an attempt to gain statistically significant results of protein expression.

Graph of induced vs uninduced fluorescence readings of various cultures, with data in quadruplets. 1,2 and 3 are transformant cultures. Joshi is a curli expressing strain we received from the Joshi lab, intended as a positive control. Lb is a negative control of LB broth.

This assay supported our positive control expressing curli, but indicated to us that our transformants were struggling to express curli. Given that our transformants were confirmed by sequencing, we suspected protein folding issues. Using Alphafold 3, we modeled the oligomerization of our modified curli fiber as compared to our positive control, and saw significant differences in folding.

To further support our hypothesis that the issue with our sample occurred in protein folding, we ran RT-PCR to confirm the transcription of our modified gene.

Carbonic Anhydrase

Following the 2023 iGEM team MSP-Maastricht that used sazCA, we performed the Wilbur-Anderson assay to investigate carbonic anhydrase activity. The results indicated that we had limited cabonic anhydrase activity, likely due to protein aggregation as shown in alphafold (Too be updated when we rerun this assay).

S. Pastureii Biomineralization

As biomineralization is a key part of our project, we decided to use naturally biomineralizing S. pasteurii to make a material. S. pasteurii uses the urealytic pathway to biomineralize, turning urea into ammonia and bicarbonate. When Ca2+ is added to the solution, calcium carbonate mineralizes around the bacteria. We confirmed biomineralization by comparing S. pasteurii in CMM- media against CMM- media under a microscope

Material Creation and Characterization

To estimate the strength of our biomineralized material, we created mold following ASTM standards for tensile/compressive strength testing, but scaled all dimensions down. The resulting material had a cross-sectional area of 2 squared, allowing us to estimate tensile strength.

Samples were then taken out of molds and sterilized as per our sterilization protocol [link to experiment page, sterilization protocol]. We were able to confirm that our protocol successfully sterilized our samples via a swab test. While the process of removing the samples from our molds broke the mineralized material into multiple pieces rendering us unable to measure strength, we can see that the sand has clearly turned into a much more coarse material.
